René Brar, Co-founder Soshefeigh

René Brar is co-founder of Soshefeigh, a Vancouver-based production company that specializes in creating TV formats that travel the world. Soshefeigh has launched or sold original shows in the US, Canada and throughout Europe, including the comedy series Battle Cats and “big data” game show The Algorithm.

Before stepping out on his own, René cut his teeth as a story editor for filmmakers Ridley and Tony Scott on such films as Black Hawk Down, Matchstick Men, and Spy Game. More recently, René directed and produced indie feature films Taylor’s Way and Martin’s Pink Pickle.

Q: How did you get into film and making movies?

My dad showed me a lot of important movies at a young age and that first got me hooked. In high school, I always had a camera in hand. When I went to college I studied film, made shorts, and pretty much only talked about movies.


Q: You spent a lot of time in Hollywood and worked on some pretty major movies, what was that like?

Equal parts thrilling and terrifying. It was a great experience and it taught me a lot about the movies that I want – and don’t want – to make.


Q. Your first feature length film Taylor’s Way was received to much acclaim; can you tell us about it?

Making that film was thrilling and terrifying too, but for very different reasons. There’s nothing like doing it on your own – raising the money, putting together the team, directing the shit out of a scene. We travelled the world with that film and picked-up a lot of awards, but the thing that sticks with me the most was the time we spent making it. A lot of fun.


Q. I know that you’ve recently released your second film Martin’s Pink Pickle - what’s it about and how was the experience directing and producing your second film different from the first?

It’s an anti-romantic comedy about a young couple that’s late for an abortion. We shot this movie when I first moved back to Vancouver from Los Angeles and it was a great way to re-acquaint myself with home. I learned a lot of new things about my city during that film. That’s why I love shooting on location – it forces you to explore the corners you usually avoid.


Q. Are there any projects in development or in the pipeline that you can share with us?

The biggest project is my new production company, Soshefeigh, which I launched with my friend and colleague Matt O’Brien. We’ve been busy creating new television formats and producing them all over the place – Vancouver, Los Angeles, London, Dublin…

It’s been a tremendous amount of work and meant a lot of travelling – but it’s also been a lot of fun!


Q. Who or what do you count among your influences as a filmmaker and the question that everyone will want to know the answer to, if you had to pick your three favorite movies of all time, what would they be?

Robert Bresson’s book “Notes on the Cinematographer” is huge for me. Also... Italian cinema, concert films, and Barry Lyndon.


Q. How are you using Shoot to help you run your business?

While a great stories, acting and directing are a huge part of making a successful film or show, distribution and promotion of a film once it’s finished is just as important.  A lot of my work includes attending festivals and going to a lot of the international media markets like Cannes, London and Los Angeles. There are days when we literally take 15 back-to-back 30-minute meetings – with broadcasters, financiers, other producers – and there’s a ton of info that needs to be exchanged or stored and it’s impossible to do any kind of formal info exchange with everyone. With Shoot I can email anyone literally in seconds, they get all my details and I get their email. Hollywood is a business just like any other and being able to bring all the right people together at the right time makes all the difference and Shoot helps me do that.
